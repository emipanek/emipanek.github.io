    Retrieval analysis in the method used to infer the physical and chemical properties of an exoplanet’s atmosphere from observed data. The goal is to reverse engineer the observed spectrum by exploring a wide range of possible atmospheric models to find the best fit to the data. This process involves a lot of uncertainty because there can be many combinations of atmospheric properties that can produce similar spectra. Bayesian statistics provides a framework to handle this uncertainty systematically by using probability distributions to describe the likelihood of different models and parameters.

    Bayesian statistics

    Uses Bayes' Theorem to update the probability of a hypothesis given evidence.

    \[ P(\theta |D)= \frac{P(D|\theta)P(\theta)}{P(D)} \]

    • P(θ|D): Posterior probability
    • P(D|θ): Likelihood
    • P(θ): Prior probability
    • P(D): Marginal likelihood
    the likelihood, which tells us how likely the observed data D are given a particular set of parameters θ. It is typically calculated by comparing the model predictions to the observed data. P(θ) is the prior probability distribution, representing what we knew or assumed about the parameters before seeing the data. For example, we might have prior knowledge that water vapor is likely present in the atmosphere, or we might assume a range of possible temperatures. P(D) is the evidence or marginal likelihood, which is the probability of the data, irrespective of the specific model parameters. It is calculated by integrating the likelihood over all possible parameter values.

    Key Concepts in Bayesian Inference

    • Priors: Initial belief or information about a parameter before data is observed.
    • Likelihood: How probable the observed data is under a specific hypothesis.
    • Posterior: Updated belief after observing the data.
    • Marginal Likelihood: Overall probability of the observed data under all hypotheses.

    Prior vs Posterior

    The **prior** is your belief before seeing the data, and the **posterior** is the updated belief after seeing the data.

    This illustrates how evidence adjusts your initial beliefs.

    Evidence

    \[ P(D) = \int P(D|\theta)P(\theta) d\theta \]

    integral over all the information you have about a problem

    Bayes factor

    \[ B = \frac{P(D;M_2)}{P(D;M_1)} \]

    allows us to pick which model will best describe the data

    The evidence or marginal likelihood P(D) plays an important role in model comparison. When comparing different models, the Bayesian evidence tells us which model is more likely given the data The Bayes factor compares two models by computing the ratio of their evidence

    Nested Sampling

    1. Start with a set of random samples in the parameter space.
    2. Iteratively discard points with low likelihood and replace them with points of higher likelihood.
    3. Continue until the evidence is accurately estimated.

    Credits: Buchner 2023

    Nested sampling is a statistical technique used to compute Bayesian evidence and explore the posterior distribution efficiently. 1. Nested sampling begins by randomly sampling points from the prior distribution. These points represent potential combinations of model parameters 2. It then iteratively removes the points with the lowest likelihood and replaces them with new points that have higher likelihoods, but still satisfy the prior constraints. 3. As the algorithm progresses, it keeps track of the shrinking volume of parameter space that is being explored and estimates the evidence by summing the likelihoods at each stage. good balance between exploration and precision

    When to Use Nested Sampling?

    • When the posterior distribution is multi-modal or has complex shapes.
    • When Bayesian evidence (marginal likelihood) is required for model comparison.
    • Effective for problems with high-dimensional parameter spaces.

    The Rossiter-McLaughlin effect occurs when an object, blocks part of a rotating star as it crosses in front of it. This phenomenon isn't unique to exoplanets and was originally observed in eclipsing binary star systems. In our case, as the planet blocks the portion of the star that’s rotating toward us, the overall starlight becomes less blue-shifted. And when it blocks the part of the star rotating away, the starlight becomes less red-shifted. This creates a detectable distortion in the star’s spectral lines

    And what if there is disequilibrium chemistry?

    disequilibrium chemistry arises when the actual chemical composition deviates from this thermodynamic balance. Disequilibrium can occur due to external influences or when chemical reactions are unable to keep up with rapidly changing conditions. Two key mechanisms that can lead to disequilibrium in planetary atmospheres are quenching and photochemistry. Quenching refers to a situation where vertical mixing in an atmosphere transports gases between regions of different temperatures and pressures faster than chemical reactions can adjust to reach equilibrium. Photochemistry refers to chemical reactions that are driven by the absorption of high-energy photons, particularly ultraviolet (UV) light from a star.

    Part 4: What are the potential limitations?

    Although our techniques are powerful, it’s important to acknowledge the limitations of current methods and technologies. This brings us to the final part of my presentation: what challenges do we face in exoplanet transmission spectroscopy, and what innovations are on the horizon to overcome them?

    1D/3D bias

    One limitation we face is the simplification of exoplanet atmospheres in our models. Most models assume that atmospheres are uniform and spherical, but in reality, they are three-dimensional systems with complex weather patterns and temperature variations. This introduces uncertainties that can complicate our interpretations.

    1D/3D bias

    • Collaboration to characterize the bias from 3D effect

      • GJ 1214b, warm sub-Neptune, Teq = 600 K

      HD 189733b, Hot Jupiter, Teq = 1200 K

      WASP-121b, Ultra Hot Jupiter, Teq = 2400 K

    • Details in Jaziri et al. 2024
    600 K (cooler planets): Longer radiative timescales lead to more global, uniform circulation patterns, with weaker zonal flows and no pronounced equatorial jet. 1200 K (moderate temperatures): Radiative timescales and wave activity are balanced, driving strong day-night temperature contrasts and the formation of an equatorial jet through superrotation. 2400 K (hotter planets): Short radiative timescales and intense heating create highly chaotic, turbulent atmospheres where organized equatorial jets may not form.
